Case Study · Brain in Hand · 2023

From manual chaos to a single source of truth

A B2B SaaS platform serving 25,000 neurodivergent individuals across UK universities and local authorities had a data problem. The real cost was carried by the people who had to work around it every day.

Role

Lead Product Designer

Sector

B2B SaaS · Higher Education · Local Government

Team

PM, Business Analyst, Dev Team, CST Lead

Timeline

March – September 2023 · 6 months

The Context

The tools had multiplied.
The truth had not.

Brain in Hand is a regulated, cloud-based platform used by universities and local authorities to support neurodivergent individuals through daily life. When someone is referred to the service, a 90-day registration window opens - and the Customer Service Team must coordinate that user's journey across multiple systems with no automated status tracking.

Data had grown organically across three separate platforms. None of them talked to each other. Changing privacy laws required migrating user data from US-hosted servers to UK infrastructure - a compliance imperative that made the existing architecture unsustainable.

The core problem wasn't a lack of data. It was that the data was invisible and disconnected - and the people who needed it most were paying the price.

What I Found

The CST weren't inefficient.
The system had failed them.

I didn't rely on process documentation. I spent several sessions shadowing the CST as they managed active user registrations in real time - watching their screens, listening to how they described their work, noting where friction was so normalised they'd stopped seeing it.

What I witnessed was a team of highly skilled professionals who had built a sophisticated system of workarounds: personal spreadsheets for tracking 90-day windows, sticky notes for case status, manual calculations repeated every time someone checked a user's progress. This wasn't inefficiency. It was competence meeting an inadequate system.

Product Context

The CST had built a sophisticated manual workaround system - spreadsheets, sticky notes, calculated countdowns. This was their actual workflow.

Design Principle

The workarounds aren't problems to design around - they are precise requirements that no PRD had captured. Design for them explicitly.

Before Any Screen Was Drawn

The design work that nobody sees - but everything depends on.

In B2B SaaS, the temptation is to jump to interface design. The real work here came first: understanding the data, mapping the system architecture, and creating a shared foundation that any interface would be built on.

I worked with our Business Analyst to cross-reference data from all three platforms, building a unified data model - the blueprint for what a single source of truth would actually contain, and the requirement for GDPR-compliant UK data migration.

Content mapping grouped every data point a CST member might need into four logical clusters - registration, referral, package, and support - which directly informed the new information architecture.

Product Context

The data model had to be clean before the interface could be trustworthy. Weeks mapping data with the BA weren't a precursor to the design - they were design.

Design Principle

Cross-functional collaboration at this level of technical depth is what makes an interface genuinely reliable. The IA is only as good as the data beneath it.

The Design

One profile. Everything a CST member needs.

The unified user profile was designed to be the single source of truth - a hub where CST members could see everything and take action without switching tools. Two views served two distinct modes of working.

Status at a Glance

Quick View

Born from a single piece of CST feedback: "I need to see registration status without opening the full profile." A light-touch overlay surfacing the 90-day countdown, case owner, registration status, and key actions - eliminating the most frequent manual lookup task in seconds.

Case Management

Full Profile

The single source of truth. Organised into four sections - user summary, registration timeline, package details, and support history - in the order CST members told us they actually moved through a case. Edits happen within modals to keep users in context.

Three decisions worth naming.

Accessibility

WCAG-compliant by default. Given Brain in Hand's neurodivergent user base, colour contrast was validated against WCAG standards throughout. Accessibility wasn't an audit step - it was a design input from day one.

Technical Scoping

Drawers explored, modals chosen. I explored drawers as an alternative for in-context editing. Modals were chosen to reduce new component development during a Vuetify upgrade period. A deliberate trade-off - not a default.

Phasing

Dashboard scoped out - intentionally. A future dashboard view showing case ownership, outstanding tasks, and countdown summaries was explored and deferred due to timeline. The data model we built fully supports it. A visible decision, not a default.

Validation

The hypothesis: a unified profile would eliminate manual status tracking.

✓ Validated

Quick View understood immediately. CST members could check registration status in seconds. The interaction model required no explanation.

✓ Validated

90-day counter eliminated manual calculation. The single most time-consuming daily task was completely eliminated. Every participant noticed this without prompting.

△ Refined

Multi-field editing needed refinement. Some users wanted to edit multiple fields in a single session. This informed an iteration grouping related fields more consistently within editing modals.

△ Refined

Profile–ticket link not immediately obvious. The connection between user profiles and support tickets wasn't immediately visible - a documented requirement for the future FreshDesk integration phase.

What Changed

From chaos to clarity - and a foundation built to last.

  • Manual status tracking eliminatedCST members no longer maintain personal spreadsheets or sticky notes for registration countdowns. The three-system lookup that defined the old workflow no longer exists.
  • GDPR-compliant data foundationUK data migration resolved the compliance requirement that had made the existing architecture unsustainable.
  • White-label readyThe model supports white-labelled instances for individual university and local authority clients.
  • Foundation for the dashboardThe data model fully supports the case management dashboard the CST identified as their highest-priority unmet need.

What I Carry Forward

The things this project made me certain about

01

The dashboard I should have fought harder for

Every discovery conversation pointed toward a higher-level view - case ownership, outstanding tasks, days remaining across all active registrations. If I were starting again, I'd advocate for even a minimal viable dashboard in the initial scope.

02

Shadowing is non-negotiable in internal tool design

Jo's quote shaped the entire project - and I only heard it because I was in the room watching real work happen. The workarounds CST members had built weren't problems to design around - they were precise requirements that no PRD had captured.

03

Data modelling with a BA is design work

The unified profile only works because the underlying data model is clean. The weeks mapping data weren't a precursor to the design - they were design. Cross-functional collaboration at that depth is what enables an interface to be genuinely trustworthy.

04

Trade-offs should be visible, not defaults

Parking the dashboard, deferring email integration, choosing modals over drawers - these were the right calls for delivery. What matters is that they were deliberate decisions, documented and communicated, not things that just fell out of scope.
