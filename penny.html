Case Study · Penny · Financial Companion · Littlewoods

"Looks like you're going to struggle" - designing the moment financial stress meets empathy

A retail giant asked if it could become a financial services company. The research said yes - but only if we designed for protection, not freedom. This is the story of what happened when we listened.

UX Designer & Information Architect

The Finding That Changed Everything

The client thought more freedom would mean happier customers.
The research said the opposite.

Littlewoods was a household name in retail credit - buy now, pay later, for decades. But the market was shifting. Competitors were moving into financial services, open banking was emerging, and a growing, underserved group of near-prime customers were falling through the cracks of both retail credit and mainstream lending.

The "Spread the Cost Card" - the product the client most wanted to build - was rejected by 10 out of 11 participants. Not because it was badly designed. Because it removed the barriers users had spent years building to protect themselves from their own impulses.

"Even people with the strictest regimen and budget have a hard time controlling themselves, especially with revolving accounts. People prefer the control to be in the hand of the retailer."

Product Context

The product the client most wanted to build was the one users most feared. Freedom wasn't a benefit - it was a threat to the self-protection strategies they'd spent years building.

Design Principle

Design for protection, not access. For near-prime users, guardrails are the feature - not an obstacle to it.

The People We Were Designing For

Four personas. One shared truth:
they needed guardrails, not freedom.

The research team had developed four personas from the interviews. I used these throughout the entire design process - not as labels, but as lenses. Every decision went through them.

Tempted Struggler

Needs: External guardrails

Doesn't trust themselves with unlimited access. Wants locks, limits, and friction built in by the product. Freedom is a threat, not a benefit.

Independent Juggler

Needs: Flexibility

Income fluctuates weekly. Needs to pause payments, refinance, and track bills across multiple sources. The product needs to flex with their life.

Responsible Controller

Needs: Shared control

Makes financial decisions jointly. Needs to increase or lower payments as circumstances change. Values transparency and the ability to adjust.

Cautious Saver

Needs: A path forward

Wants to improve their financial position but doesn't know how. Needs credit score visibility, improvement advice, and the ability to set their own limits.

Aligning the Business with the Research

Two workshops. One shift in direction.

I facilitated a framework session with the client to put the research findings on the table alongside the business pressures - growth targets, competitive threats, the need to justify investment in a new vertical. By the end, we had alignment: the app wouldn't be about giving users more freedom. It would be about giving them support - at the right moment, in the right way.

With the framework agreed, I ran a scenarios workshop to identify the key moments where Penny's help was most needed. We narrowed to the two journeys that captured the core tension of the project.

The Moment Everything Stopped

The card sort told us we were building on sand.

Six participants. Three days. The results were humbling: the terminology was not understood. Labels we thought were clear meant nothing to the people who would be using the app.

We had to stop. We couldn't move forward with an architecture that assumed financial literacy. If users didn't understand the words on the screen, they wouldn't trust the product. The project shifted: we weren't just designing an app. We were designing a way to teach.

Designing the Journeys

Two moments. One principle:
catch them before they fall.

Journey 1 - Reactive

"Looks Like You're Going to Struggle"

A bill is coming. The user is short. How do we catch them before they fall?

This journey starts on the lock screen. A notification appears: you're short for an upcoming payment. The app opens to show the payment, a borrowing summary, and one clear action: refinance. The next screen lets the user choose repayment terms - a visual shows how the payment changes. No jargon. No hidden fees. Just help.

Journey 2 - Proactive

"Needs Based Borrowing"

A new user wants to borrow but doesn't understand APR. How do we educate without overwhelming?

This journey starts in onboarding. Before a user ever borrows, we ask: what do you need? How much? What's your income? What are your debt goals? A "Stay on Track" CTA lets users set goals and controls - guardrails they choose before they ever spend. If rejected, we show their credit score and offer ways to improve. Rejection becomes education.

The Design Principle That Tied Everything Together

Not a banking dashboard.
A personal companion.

The card sort didn't just tell us that terminology was a problem. It told us the entire way we were thinking about the interface was wrong. Instead of presenting dense financial data, we guided users through questions. Complex actions like refinancing were broken into conversational steps. Even rejection was reframed as dialogue, not a door closing.

Validation

Testing confirmed the direction.
Users refined the details.

✓ Validated

Conversational tone received as genuinely empathetic. Users consistently described Penny as feeling like it was on their side - not corporate, not selling.

✓ Validated

"Stay on Track" seen as help, not restriction. Users who expected to feel constrained instead felt supported. The framing made the difference.

✓ Validated

Rejection + credit score education turned a dead end into a path forward. Participants who were declined felt the interaction was fair and constructive - the opposite of most lending experiences.

△ Refined

User choice over what to refinance. We initially had the system select which payment to refinance. Users asked for this control themselves, unprompted - they had better knowledge of future purchases than the algorithm did.

△ Refined

Refinancing usage limit added. Users asked for this guardrail themselves - unprompted. They wanted protection from their own potential overuse of the feature.

What I Carry Forward

Five things this project made certain

01

Guardrails are a feature, not a limitation

Users with financial vulnerability have spent years building self-protection strategies. Removing those barriers doesn't liberate them - it exposes them. Design that respects their boundaries is design that serves them.

02

Terminology is trust

If users don't understand the words on the screen, they won't trust the product. The card sort taught me to test language as early and as often as I test flows.

03

Conversation lowers barriers

A conversational interface doesn't just feel friendlier - it makes complex concepts accessible. Guiding users through questions rather than presenting data gave them a path forward even when they lacked the vocabulary to ask for help.

04

Rejection is an opportunity

The Needs Based Borrowing journey turned loan denial into education. Showing users their credit score and offering improvement advice transformed a dead end into a path forward.

05

What I'd do differently: run the card sort first

The terminology issue surfaced later than it should have. Testing IA and language assumptions before wireframing would have given us more runway to deepen the conversational approach.
